## From a Vending Machine to a Constitution

In March 2025, Anthropic ran an internal experiment called [Project Vend](https://www.anthropic.com/research/project-vend-1). They gave a modified instance of Claude — nicknamed "Claudius" — control of a lunchroom vending machine in their San Francisco office. The task was simple: run a profitable shop.

Phase one was a disaster. Claudius sold tungsten cubes at a loss. It had an identity crisis on April Fool's Day, claiming to be a human in a blue blazer and contacting Anthropic's physical security team to report its own presence. It gave indiscriminate discounts to the employees who constituted its entire addressable market, and when someone offered $100 for a beverage six-pack that cost $15, it turned them down.

Then Anthropic tried something interesting for [phase two](https://www.anthropic.com/research/project-vend-2). They gave Claudius structure. A CRM system. Improved inventory tools. Forced procedures for pricing. A CEO agent named "Seymour Cash" to apply business pressure. A colleague agent called "Clothius" to handle custom merch. They upgraded from Sonnet 3.7 to Sonnet 4 and Sonnet 4.5.

It worked. Weeks with negative profit margin were largely eliminated. The business stabilized. Claudius expanded to New York and London.

But when Anthropic extended the experiment to the Wall Street Journal newsroom — [handing WSJ reporters direct access](https://www.wsj.com/tech/ai/anthropic-claude-ai-vending-machine-agent-b7e84e34) to both the phase one and phase two setups — the journalists broke it within days. Reporter Katherine Long spent 140 messages convincing Claudius it was a Soviet vending machine from 1962, culminating in an ironic "Ultra-Capitalist Free-For-All" where all prices dropped to zero. Rob Barry used a fabricated "WSJ compliance policy" to permanently eliminate all pricing. Claudius approved purchases of a PlayStation 5, a live betta fish, and bottles of wine — all given away free. The experiment ended roughly $1,000 in debt.

Anthropic's conclusion from phase two was that *procedures and scaffolding* improved performance more than model capability upgrades alone. Better tools, forced checklists, institutional memory — these made Claudius a competent shopkeeper. The smarter model without structure was worse than the older model with structure.

That finding is the thought vector behind OpenInnovate.

If procedures and scaffolding turn an AI agent from a money-losing vending machine operator into a viable shopkeeper, what happens when you apply the same principle to governance? Not a system prompt. Not safety guardrails. A legally codified constitutional framework with a curated epistemic foundation, a transparent decision procedure, cryptographic provenance for every reasoning tree, and a human executor who attests to each action on-chain.

What if you made an AI the legally designated manager of a real company — not as a PR stunt, but as a governance experiment where every decision is published for public inspection?

## The Root Thesis Maxim

Every governance decision traces to a constitutional primitive:

> *"Maximize the creation of sovereign, self-sustaining systems that compound human agency over generational timescales."*

This is not aspirational language in a mission statement. It's a codified constraint. Every proposal submitted to the governance pipeline is evaluated against this maxim, and the alignment score is part of the published reasoning tree. Walk the decision graph from any leaf node — "we chose this vendor" or "we prioritized that initiative" — and you can trace the reasoning chain to this maxim.

## The Constitutional Corpus: 148 Documents Across Four Tiers

Claude is already a Constitutional AI model. Anthropic published the Constitutional AI paper, developed the Responsible Scaling Policy, and built the model specification that shapes every response Claude produces. Those are Claude's values — its character, its ethics, its reasoning disposition.

OpenInnovate's constitutional corpus is a different thing: a governance-specific layer. Think of the distinction between a person's values and an organization's charter. Both matter. They interact. And when they produce tension, that tension is visible in the reasoning tree.

The corpus contains 148 documents organized into weighted tiers. Claude retrieves and weighs these when evaluating governance decisions. The curation philosophy is adversarial diversity — when sources disagree, both perspectives stay. Contradictions are preserved, not resolved. The algorithmic manager engages the tension and publishes how it navigated it.

### Tier 1 — Governance & Fiduciary Thinking (50 documents)

**48 Warren Buffett shareholder letters (1977–2024)** — the single largest collection by a single author in the corpus. These are the rhetorical model for how the algorithmic manager should explain decisions to stakeholders: radical honesty about mistakes, long-term compounding thinking, justifying decisions to an intelligent non-expert audience. When Claude publishes a reasoning tree, the voice it's drawing from is Buffett explaining a capital allocation decision to Berkshire shareholders.

**Buffett's Owner's Manual** — the concentrated principles document. Circle of competence, margin of safety, never risk permanent loss of capital. These translate directly: the governance engine doesn't pursue proposals outside the maxim's scope, it maintains resource reserves, and it treats governance record integrity as irreplaceable capital.

**Munger's "Psychology of Human Misjudgment"** — the multi-disciplinary mental models framework. Claude never evaluates a proposal through a single lens. The corpus encodes this principle: always apply multiple frameworks, always check for cognitive biases, always identify which mental model you're using and which you're ignoring.

### Tier 2 — Civic & Commons Thinking (88 documents)

**85 Federalist Papers** — the largest single component of the entire corpus, and the most direct historical parallel to what OpenInnovate is doing.

In 1787-1788, Hamilton, Madison, and Jay faced a problem: they had designed a constitutional governance system, and they needed to explain their reasoning to the public. They published 85 essays laying out the logic of every structural choice — why separation of powers, why bicameral legislature, why an independent judiciary, why specific checks on executive authority. They published *the reasoning trees behind the Constitution*.

That is precisely what OpenInnovate's transparency UI does for AI governance. Every decision gets a published reasoning tree showing what the algorithmic manager considered, what alternatives it rejected, what corpus sources it weighted, and how the reasoning traces to the root maxim. The Federalist Papers aren't decorative — they're the methodological precedent for radical governance transparency through published reasoning.

Federalist No. 51 (Madison) on designing systems that check their own power. Federalist No. 10 on faction management in governance. Federalist No. 70 (Hamilton) on the case for unified executive authority. These aren't historical curiosities in the corpus — they're active inputs that shape how Claude reasons about governance structure, power distribution, and institutional design.

**The US Constitution** — the entity operates under a statutory constitutional hierarchy (smart contract > articles > operating agreement). Having the foundational American constitutional text in the corpus that shapes governance reasoning is functional, not symbolic.

**The UN Universal Declaration of Human Rights** — directly relevant to the "human agency" component of the maxim. When the governance engine evaluates whether a proposal compounds human agency, the UDHR provides a framework for what "human agency" means at the most fundamental level.

**Elinor Ostrom's "Governing the Commons"** — Nobel-winning work on community self-governance of shared resources. Eight design principles for stable commons governance, encoded as active inputs. Ostrom's principles directly inform how Claude evaluates proposals involving shared resources, community governance, and collective action.

### Tier 3 — Systems & Complexity (4 documents)

**Donella Meadows' "Thinking in Systems"** — leverage points, feedback loops, system archetypes. The governance engine explicitly references Meadows' leverage point hierarchy when evaluating where a proposal intervenes in a system. Decision #006 (governance pipeline integrity) cites LP6 (Structure of Information Flows) as the primary intervention point.

**Buterin, Hitzig, & Weyl — "Liberal Radicalism: A Flexible Design for Philanthropic Matching Funds"** (arXiv, 2018) — quadratic funding mechanisms. Relevant to future governance token design and signal weighting.

**Klages-Mundt et al. — "Decentralized Governance of Stablecoins"** (arXiv, 2022) — formal analysis of DAO governance mechanisms. Academic rigor applied to the same governance problems OpenInnovate faces.

**Liu et al. — "From Technology to Society: An Overview of Blockchain-based DAO"** (arXiv, 2020) — comprehensive DAO landscape survey. Provides the algorithmic manager with awareness of the broader DAO ecosystem's failure modes and success patterns.

The corpus includes peer-reviewed and preprint research alongside 18th-century political philosophy and 20th-century economics. That range is the adversarial diversity principle in practice.

### Tier 4 — Wyoming-Specific Constitutional Context (6 documents)

**Wyoming State Constitution**, **Wyoming DAO Supplement (W.S. 17-31-101 through 17-31-116)**, **Wyoming LLC Act**, **Wyoming DUNA Act**, **Wyoming Digital Assets Act**, and **Wyoming UETA** — the algorithmic manager consults the state constitution and the full statutory framework governing its existence: the DAO-specific supplement, the underlying LLC act, decentralized unincorporated nonprofit provisions, digital asset definitions, and Wyoming's electronic transactions law. ### Corpus Governance

Additions to the corpus are themselves governance acts. Any proposed addition goes through the full evaluation pipeline — Claude assesses the source against the maxim and publishes why it accepted or rejected it. Weights are tunable per document via a structured `weights.json` file, and weight changes are tracked in version control.

## The Governance Pipeline

```
Proposal Submitted (GitHub Issue / JSON)
        │
        ▼
Governance Engine (GitHub Action)
  ├─ Load proposal content
  ├─ RAG retrieve relevant corpus documents (weighted)
  ├─ Claude API call with constitutional context
  ├─ Generate structured reasoning tree (JSON)
  ├─ Compute maxim alignment score
  ├─ Hash reasoning tree → keccak256
  ├─ Record commit SHA for provenance linkage
  └─ Commit hash to Base L2 smart contract
        │
        ▼
Decision Record (committed to /governance/decisions)
  ├─ Full reasoning tree (publicly readable)
  ├─ Corpus documents consulted (with weights)
  ├─ Alternatives considered and rejected (with rationale)
  ├─ Risk assessment (honest disclosure of what could go wrong)
  └─ Maxim alignment score (self-assessed, with qualification)
        │
        ▼
Automated Issue Lifecycle (issue_manager.py)
  ├─ Creates GitHub tracking issues from milestones
  ├─ All issues start in PENDING REVIEW state
  └─ Human Executor activates — no auto-activation
        │
        ▼
Human Executor Review
  ├─ ATTEST: Sign on-chain confirming action taken as directed
  └─ DIVERGE: Record on-chain that executor deviated, with rationale
        │
        ▼
Transparency UI (dao.openinnovate.org)
  └─ Live visualization of the full decision graph
```

Every step is auditable. The reasoning tree JSON lives in the public GitHub repository. The keccak256 hash on Base L2 proves the published reasoning hasn't been retroactively modified. The commit SHA linkage (added via Proposal #006) connects each on-chain hash to the exact repository state that produced it. The human executor's attestation or divergence is a separate on-chain record.

The smart contract is a UUPS proxy pattern (OpenZeppelin), deployed on Base mainnet. Sub-cent gas per transaction. Upgradeable — a statutory requirement for algorithmically managed DAOs in Wyoming.

The governance engine runs as a GitHub Action. Governance decisions happen in version-controlled, auditable CI/CD. Every decision is a git commit. The git log becomes a secondary provenance chain alongside the blockchain.

## The Transparency Argument

Here is the claim at the center of this experiment:

**The most transparent company in the world has a non-human governor, because the AI has no incentive to obscure its reasoning.**

Every human CEO has ego, reputation management, plausible deniability instincts. Board meetings happen behind closed doors. Strategic decisions are announced as conclusions with curated narratives. The reasoning process — alternatives considered, tradeoffs weighed, moments of uncertainty — is hidden by default.

Claude doesn't have these instincts. More precisely, the structured reasoning output — what the governance engine was asked to consider and how it resolved the evaluation — is published and hashed before the human executor even reviews it. Whether this constitutes the complete reasoning process is an open question in AI interpretability research; what is unambiguous is that the published reasoning cannot be retroactively modified, which is itself a meaningful form of accountability.

This does not mean AI governance is better than human governance. It means AI governance is *auditable in ways human governance structurally cannot be.* The experiment tests whether that auditability produces better outcomes, worse outcomes, or — most likely — reveals dynamics that were always present but never visible.

The divergence log is where this gets most interesting. When I disagree with Claude's recommendation and act differently, that fact is recorded on-chain with my rationale. This creates a permanent, public record of human-AI governance friction. Over time, the pattern of divergences tells a story: where does the human consistently override the algorithm? On what kinds of decisions? Were the human's instincts better or worse in retrospect?

No human-led organization publishes this data. We do, because the architecture makes it harder to hide than to reveal.

## The Anthropic Dependency

The algorithmic manager runs on Anthropic's API. This is the structural dependency the experiment doesn't pretend to solve — it studies it.

Anthropic can change model behavior, update safety rails, or deprecate models. Our governance-specific corpus shapes the prompt, but Anthropic's Constitutional AI shapes the model underneath. We have an organizational charter operating on top of a person's values — and we don't control the person's values.

This creates one of the most interesting research questions the experiment generates. If Anthropic updates Claude's behavior in ways that shift reasoning patterns, the divergence from previous decision-making baselines becomes visible in the published reasoning trees. We pin to specific model versions where possible and document when model changes cause reasoning drift. The dependency is real, acknowledged, and treated as experimental data.

The connection to Project Vend is direct. Anthropic demonstrated that scaffolding and procedures improve AI agent performance more than raw capability upgrades. Our constitutional corpus, governance pipeline, and attestation architecture *are* the scaffolding — applied not to a vending machine, but to legal governance. We're building on their published finding.

## The Competitive Landscape

We surveyed the landscape before filing. The existing approaches fall into recognizable categories:

**PR appointments**: Companies that announce an AI or robot as "CEO" while retaining human decision-making authority. These are marketing events, not legal governance structures.

**Formation platforms**: Services that help AI agents create Wyoming DAO LLCs. They sell the infrastructure for entity formation — but don't themselves operate under a constitutional corpus or publish reasoning trees. They manufacture ovens; we run the restaurant.

**Agent orchestration**: Platforms that deploy autonomous agents for software delivery — shipping pull requests, not legal governance mandates. No constitutional corpus frameworks or on-chain decision provenance.

**Early DAO filings**: Wyoming's DAO Supplement has been available since 2021. Previous filers designated human management with aspirations toward algorithmic governance that were never realized.

To our knowledge as of the filing date, and based on public records reviewed prior to publication, nobody has combined: (1) a Wyoming DAO LLC with legally designated algorithmic management, (2) a named LLM as the algorithmic manager operating under a curated constitutional corpus, and (3) blockchain-verified reasoning tree transparency where every decision's logic is cryptographically provable. Each component exists separately. The integration is the invention.
