# Leverage Points: Places to Intervene in a System
# Author: Donella H. Meadows
# Source: The Sustainability Institute, 1999
# Original publication: Whole Earth, Winter 1997
# Retrieved: 2026-03-24
# Status: Freely available paper, widely republished

Donella Meadows identified twelve places to intervene in a complex system, ordered from least to most effective. Her insight was counterintuitive: the interventions people most commonly attempt (adjusting parameters, tweaking numbers) are the least effective, while the most powerful interventions (changing paradigms, transcending paradigms) are the hardest to execute but transform systems fundamentally.

This framework is directly relevant to governance: most governance acts operate at the lower leverage points (adjusting rules, changing numbers). Truly transformative governance operates at the higher leverage points (restructuring information flows, changing goals, shifting paradigms).

The twelve leverage points, from LEAST to MOST effective:


12. CONSTANTS, PARAMETERS, NUMBERS
(such as subsidies, taxes, standards)

These are the dials and settings of a system. Adjusting them rarely changes behavior because the system has usually adapted to absorb parameter changes. A tax rate change, a subsidy adjustment, a regulatory threshold — these are the interventions that politicians and managers spend most of their time on, yet they produce the least systemic change. Parameters become leverage points only when they push a system across a threshold or tipping point.


11. THE SIZES OF BUFFERS AND OTHER STABILIZING STOCKS, RELATIVE TO THEIR FLOWS

Buffers are stocks — inventories, reservoirs, savings — that absorb fluctuations. A large buffer stabilizes a system but makes it inflexible. A small buffer makes a system responsive but fragile. The leverage is in right-sizing buffers: enough to absorb shocks, not so much that the system becomes sluggish. Often buffers are physical and hard to change, making this a low-leverage point in practice.


10. THE STRUCTURE OF MATERIAL STOCKS AND FLOWS
(such as transport networks, population age structures, rate of soil degradation)

Physical infrastructure constrains behavior. The structure of roads determines traffic patterns; the age structure of a population determines labor supply for decades. These structures change slowly — you cannot quickly rebuild a transportation network or restructure a population. Physical structure is often the least flexible part of a system, which is why it is a low-leverage intervention point despite its importance.


9. THE LENGTHS OF DELAYS, RELATIVE TO THE RATE OF SYSTEM CHANGE

Delays in feedback loops are critical determinants of system behavior. If information about consequences reaches decision-makers too slowly, they overshoot and oscillate. If market signals lag too far behind supply changes, boom-bust cycles result. Shortening critical delays is a high-leverage intervention when the delay is the bottleneck — but delays are often structural and physical, making them hard to change.


8. THE STRENGTH OF NEGATIVE FEEDBACK LOOPS, RELATIVE TO THE IMPACTS THEY ARE TRYING TO CORRECT AGAINST

Negative feedback loops are self-correcting mechanisms — thermostats, market price signals, democratic elections, judicial review. They keep a system within bounds. The leverage is in their strength relative to the forces they oppose. A weak regulatory agency facing a powerful industry is a negative feedback loop too weak to function. Strengthening negative feedback loops — making oversight bodies independent, giving regulators teeth, ensuring elections are competitive — is a moderately powerful intervention.


7. THE GAIN AROUND DRIVING POSITIVE FEEDBACK LOOPS

Positive feedback loops are self-reinforcing — compound interest, arms races, epidemic spread, network effects. Left unchecked, they drive exponential growth or collapse. The leverage is in slowing the gain — reducing the rate at which the rich get richer, the powerful get more powerful, or infection spreads. A system driven by unchecked positive feedback will eventually destroy itself. Governance systems must identify and moderate their positive feedback loops.


6. THE STRUCTURE OF INFORMATION FLOWS
(who does and does not have access to information)

Information flows determine who knows what and when. Adding or changing information flows is one of the most powerful and cost-effective interventions in a system. Making pollution data public changes polluter behavior. Making government spending transparent changes spending patterns. Making governance reasoning visible changes governance quality. Information does not change physical infrastructure, but it changes how every actor in the system behaves.

This is where radical transparency as a governance principle operates: by structuring information flows so that all governance reasoning, all decisions, and all divergences are publicly visible, the system creates accountability without requiring enforcement.


5. THE RULES OF THE SYSTEM
(such as incentives, punishments, constraints)

Rules define the scope, boundaries, and incentives of a system. They determine who has power, who is rewarded, who is punished, and what is permitted. Changing the rules changes the game. Constitutional provisions, statutes, organizational bylaws, smart contract logic — these are rule-level interventions. They are high-leverage because they restructure the incentive landscape that all actors operate within.

Smart contracts are rules encoded in executable code. The operating agreement is a rule system. The constitutional hierarchy (smart contract > articles > operating agreement) is a rule about rules.


4. THE POWER TO ADD, CHANGE, EVOLVE, OR SELF-ORGANIZE SYSTEM STRUCTURE

Self-organization is the ability of a system to restructure itself — to learn, diversify, complexify, and evolve. A system that can change its own rules, add new feedback loops, or restructure its information flows is far more resilient than one locked into a fixed structure. The UUPS upgrade pattern in smart contract design is a self-organization mechanism: it allows the governance system to evolve its own infrastructure.

The most resilient systems are those that maintain the capacity for self-organization. Suppressing self-organization (through rigid control, monoculture, or inflexibility) makes systems brittle and prone to catastrophic failure.


3. THE GOALS OF THE SYSTEM

The goal of a system — what it is optimizing for — determines where all the feedback loops, rules, and information flows point. A corporation optimizing for quarterly earnings behaves fundamentally differently from one optimizing for long-term value creation. A government optimizing for GDP growth behaves differently from one optimizing for citizen wellbeing.

Changing the goal of a system changes everything beneath it. The Root Thesis Maxim is a goal-level intervention: by defining the supreme governance directive as "maximize the creation of sovereign, self-sustaining systems that compound human agency over generational timescales," it redirects all subordinate decisions, rules, and information flows toward long-term compounding rather than short-term extraction.


2. THE MINDSET OR PARADIGM OUT OF WHICH THE SYSTEM ARISES
(its goals, power structure, rules, its culture)

Paradigms are the deepest held beliefs and assumptions out of which systems arise. The shared idea that growth is always good, that nature is a stock to be exploited, that corporations exist to maximize shareholder value — these are paradigms. They are upstream of goals, rules, and structures. Changing a paradigm changes everything downstream.

The paradigm that "governance should be transparent, algorithmically evaluated, and constitutionally constrained" is a departure from the prevailing paradigm that "governance is the prerogative of those in power, exercised behind closed doors." This paradigm shift is more powerful than any specific rule or structure within the DAO.


1. THE POWER TO TRANSCEND PARADIGMS

The highest leverage point is the capacity to operate beyond any single paradigm — to recognize that all paradigms are models, not reality, and to hold them lightly. A system (or individual, or organization) that can question its own deepest assumptions, recognize when its paradigm is failing, and shift to a new one has the ultimate adaptive advantage.

This is the meta-level: not being attached to any particular way of seeing the world, but maintaining the flexibility to see through multiple lenses and change lenses when the situation demands it. The annual governance review, the Divergence Protocol, and the constitutional corpus's commitment to adversarial diversity are all mechanisms designed to keep the system from calcifying around any single paradigm.

Meadows noted that people who operate at this level — who truly understand that no paradigm is "true" — are often perceived as crazy or dangerous, because they challenge the very foundations of shared reality. But they are also the source of all genuine transformation.
